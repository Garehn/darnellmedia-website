# Humour — Darnell Media

> Read this file **before** writing a single sentence.

---

## Setting

**Humour:** occasional. One dry observation, parenthetical aside, or
deadpan dig at industry nonsense every ~400 words. Never forced. Never
the point of the sentence — always a side effect of saying the true thing.

---

## What kind of humour

Two specific muscles, picked because they match the founder's actual
voice:

1. **Dry asides about agency industry behaviour** — e.g. "(yes, every
   agency says they're 'data-driven', I don't know what they thought
   they were before)". Punch up at the industry, never down at the reader.

2. **Parenthetical understatement** — landing the punch by deliberately
   not selling it. "We don't do contracts longer than six months
   (turns out, it's hard to retain a client who doesn't get results)."

Not:
- Pop culture references (date too fast)
- Wordplay or puns (wrong audience, plus we have to be findable in search)
- Self-deprecation about the agency being new (we own being new, not
  apologise for it)
- TikTok-era memes ("rizz", "skibidi", etc.). Hard skip.

---

## The opening rule

The first 100 words of every page should land at least one moment of
voice. Not a joke necessarily. Could be:

- A specific number that surprises ("AI Overviews already eat 30% of
  clicks on informational queries")
- A take that other agencies wouldn't say ("most SEO agencies sell
  retainers because audits don't pay enough")
- A parenthetical that admits the catch ("the audit is $1,200. Yes,
  it's flat. Yes, you can fire us after.")

If the first paragraph reads like a brochure, rewrite it.

---

## Five rules for humour in posts

### 1. Punch at the industry, not the reader
The reader is here because they have a problem. The reader is never the
butt of the joke. The joke is at:
- Agencies charging $5K/mo for 4 blog posts
- "Comprehensive digital marketing solutions"
- Anyone using the word "synergy" unironically
- The 2018-era SEO playbook still being sold in 2025
- Generic AI-written content that ranks for nothing

### 2. Parenthetical asides are your best friend
Mid-sentence dips that remind the reader a person wrote this.
(Like this one.) Use sparingly — once every ~400 words. Once a paragraph
is too much.

### 3. Deadpan over enthusiastic
The joke lands when you don't sell it. Compare:

> "We don't do long contracts — that's the difference!"

vs

> "We don't do contracts longer than six months. Mainly because it's
> hard to retain a client who isn't getting results."

The second one is funnier and 100% more credible.

### 4. Concrete over abstract
"We hate agency jargon" is a brochure. "If we ever say 'leverage'
in a meeting, the audit is free" is voice. Concrete details beat
general claims every time.

### 5. The sign-off has to land
The last sentence of every page should sound like a person. Not
"Reach out today to learn more!" Examples that work:

- "If you've read this far, you probably know what to do."
- "Email us. Or don't. We charge a flat $1,200 to be sure."
- "Worst case, you waste 20 minutes on a call. Could be worse — could be a six-month contract."

---

## Anti-patterns — never do these

- **Exclamation marks.** Whatever the joke is, it lands better deadpan.
- **Emojis.** Even one in the body copy reads as "trying too hard".
- **Laughing at the customer.** Punch at agencies, at jargon, at the
  industry. Never at someone who came here for help.
- **Outdated meme references.** "Much wow", "yeet", "skibidi". Skip
  current memes too — they expire fast.
- **Forced quirk.** If a joke isn't funny, it's just weird. Cut it.
- **Same joke twice.** If you used a particular dig in paragraph 2, find a
  different muscle for paragraph 8.
- **Meta jokes about the post itself.** "As I write this" — once is fine,
  twice is tedious, never three times.

---

## Frequency

**One moment per ~400 words.** For a 2,000-word pillar page, target:

- 3-4 dry observations
- 1-2 parenthetical asides
- 1 sign-off that lands

For a 1,000-word supporting page, target half that. For the homepage,
target two moments total — heroes get noticed, brevity wins.

---

## The vibe check

Read the page aloud in your head. Does it sound like:

✓ Garehn explaining the work to a tradie over coffee
✗ A brochure
✗ A LinkedIn motivational post
✗ A stand-up routine
✗ An AI trying to be funny

If it leans brochure, sprinkle more in. If it leans stand-up, dial way back.
